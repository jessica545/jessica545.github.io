MARKETING STRATEGY

IKEA is known for having it’s own marketing tactic called “the IKEA effect,” this tactic was creating to allow consumers to feel as if their “part of the process.” The incentive that the customer has to assemble the furniture item they’ve purchased physiologically has made them feel like “co-creators,” Studies performed by Harvard’s school of Business showed that people take more value in products that require time and effort to use. As a result, customers are able to justify paying 63% more for furniture if it requires time and energy to assemble after purchasing. For IKEA, they took this statistic and decided to profit from it by implementing it into their practices.

Distinctive branding, much like other companies, IKEA has a very distinct branding and logo. Made up of the enlarged bold letters coloured bright sunshine yellow on an entirely blue building, their branding stands out and it is noticed heavily upon customer traffic. Subconsciously, potential customers see the bold logo and become inclined to stop solely based on the first impression of the logo.

Catalog marketing, IKEA has a large presence in holiday catalogs and magazines, they set up scenes with elaborate furniture pieces and decor that are staged with the interest of family homes in mind. Captivating consumers with ideas of how easy yet creative decorating their homes could be, they promote customers to buy the full sets and all the decor shown as thats what the consumer wishes their home to be decorating like.

Marketing Mix

Product: IKEA offers a wide range of well-designed and functional furniture, home furnishings, and accessories at affordable prices. Their products are often known for their Scandinavian design aesthetic, durability, and functionality. IKEA's flat-pack furniture is crafted for self-assembly, streamlining shipping expenses and storage needs. This strategy not only cuts shipping costs for the company but also facilitates convenient transportation and assembly for customers.

Price: IKEA follows a penetration pricing strategy, offering its products at relatively low prices compared to competitors. This pricing strategy aims to attract a wide customer base and create value for customers seeking affordable yet stylish home furnishings.

Place (Distribution): IKEA operates through a network of retail stores globally. These stores are typically large and strategically located in accessible areas, often on the outskirts of major cities. Additionally, IKEA offers online shopping and delivery services to reach customers who prefer to shop from home.

Promotion: IKEA utilizes various promotional strategies to communicate its brand message and attract customers. This includes advertising through traditional channels such as television, print media, and billboards, as well as digital marketing efforts including social media, email marketing, and its website. IKEA also engages in sales promotions, such as seasonal discounts and special offers, to encourage purchases.

Market segmentation

Geography: Urban vs. suburban: IKEA might differentiate its marketing strategies and product assortments for customers living in urban areas with smaller living spaces compared to those in suburban or rural areas with larger homes. The company may also consider regional preferences and trends when designing its product offerings and marketing campaigns. For example, customers in colder climates may have different furniture and home furnishing needs compared to those in warmer climates.

Demographics: IKEA may target different age groups with specific product lines and marketing messages. For instance, younger customers might be attracted to modern and trendy furniture designs, while older customers may prefer more traditional styles. IKEA might also take into account Income level thus offering products at various price points, making it accessible to customers with different income levels. However, the company might tailor its marketing efforts and promotions to appeal to budget-conscious shoppers as well as those willing to invest in higher-end furnishings. IKEA may develop products and marketing campaigns tailored to families with children, singles and young professionals, empty nesters, or retirees, considering the unique needs and preferences of each segment.

Psychographics: IKEA may segment its market based on customers' lifestyles, values, and interests. For example, it might target environmentally conscious consumers with sustainable and eco-friendly product lines or appeal to minimalist consumers with sleek and functional designs. The company might also consider personality traits such as adventurousness, creativity, or practicality when developing marketing messages and product offerings. For instance, it might position certain products as innovative solutions for creative individuals or emphasize the durability and functionality of its products for practical-minded consumers.
